# Continual Improvement — Best Practice Policy

## Policy Principles
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.1, §2.2, §2.4.1–§2.4.2, FitSM-2 §PR14 CSI Objective -->

### 1. Vision-Aligned Improvement
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.2, §2.4.1.1 -->
All improvement initiatives shall cascade from the organizational vision. If an improvement does not contribute — even in a small way — to achieving the vision, it is probably not necessary or useful. Improvement efforts shall be linked to organizational objectives and success factors, ensuring that resources are directed toward changes that increase value for stakeholders.

### 2. Everyone's Responsibility
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.4.2.1, §2.4.1.3, FitSM-2 §PR14 CSI -->
Continual improvement is the responsibility of every person in the organization — including stakeholders, sponsors, customers, users, service delivery teams, product and service owners, and suppliers and partners. People throughout the organization shall be encouraged to record improvement ideas. The organization shall establish multiple feedback channels, formal and informal, and all feedback shall be respected and reviewed.

### 3. Safe-to-Fail Environment
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §4.2.2.2, §2.4.1.3 -->
The organization shall create and maintain an environment where it is safe to experiment and fail on a small scale. Blame culture shall be avoided — failures are opportunities to learn. Incremental, iterative improvement techniques allow improvements to fail frequently and at limited scale, thereby reducing the probability and impact of large-scale failure. Psychological safety, built on respect and trust, is a prerequisite for effective continual improvement.

### 4. Evidence-Based Decision-Making
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.4.2.5, FitSM-2 §PR14 CSI -->
Improvement initiatives shall be justified with data and evidence. Business justifications shall include costs, expected benefits, success criteria, and measurements. Prioritization criteria shall be transparent and applied impartially. The organization shall assess both return on investment and value on investment — recognizing that return on investment alone does not capture the real value of service improvement. The value of not improving (value retained by avoiding degradation) shall also be considered.

### 5. Iterative Progress
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.4.1.1, §2.4.1.2 -->
Improvement shall proceed iteratively — work divided into manageable pieces with defined goals that can be incrementally achieved. Large changes are riskier and take longer to show results; incremental changes reduce risk and enable faster learning. Working in short iterations that deliver value quickly and visibly reinforces the value that users gain from improvement work.

### 6. Learning and Knowledge Sharing
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.4.1.4, §3.2.2 Table 3.4 -->
The organization shall capture lessons learned from every improvement initiative — both from successes and from initiatives that did not achieve planned outcomes. Lessons learned shall be documented, communicated, and used for future initiatives. Knowledge sharing shall be promoted as an organizational capability, not restricted to individuals or teams. Senior managers shall actively promote a knowledge sharing culture.

### 7. Continual Improvement of Continual Improvement
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.5 -->
The continual improvement practice itself shall be subject to ongoing measurement and improvement. The organization shall assess whether its improvement methods are yielding expected results, whether the improvement culture is being embedded effectively, and whether improvement initiatives are delivering value proportionate to their investment.

## Mandatory Requirements
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.4.1–§2.4.2, §3.2.1, §3.2.2, FitSM-2 §PR14 CSI -->

### Essential (All tiers)

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 1 | A continual improvement register (CIR) shall be established and maintained to record and manage all improvement initiatives throughout their lifecycles — including unique identifier, source, owner, status, and value statement |
| 2 | All sources of potential improvement suggestions shall be identified — including staff, customers, suppliers, incident reviews, problem outcomes, service reports, audit results, and assessment findings |
| 3 | Each improvement opportunity shall be evaluated against transparent criteria — considering benefits, cost, effort, and alignment with organizational objectives — before resources are committed |
| 4 | The status and progress of all improvement actions shall be tracked through the CIR from identification through to completion and evaluation |
| 5 | The results of improvement initiatives shall be measured and evaluated against agreed success criteria, and lessons learned shall be captured |

### Intermediate (T2+)

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 6 | The organization shall establish and document a continual improvement approach — selecting methods and techniques adapted to the organization's environment and communicating this approach across the organization |
| 7 | Senior management shall actively model improvement behaviours. Job descriptions and employee objectives shall include continual improvement responsibilities. Successful improvements shall be recognized and celebrated |
| 8 | Business justifications shall be developed for improvement initiatives — proportionate to the initiative's scale — including costs, expected benefits, success criteria, and measurement methods |
| 9 | Lessons learned logs shall be maintained throughout improvement initiative implementation and reviewed to produce lessons learned reports for use in future initiatives |

### Advanced (T3)

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 10 | The continual improvement practice itself shall be periodically assessed for effectiveness — evaluating capture methods, prioritization criteria, implementation success rates, and the degree to which improvement is embedded in organizational culture |
| 11 | Suppliers and partners shall be included in continual improvement — encouraged to submit suggestions to the CIR and engaged in collaborative improvement of the supply chain |
| 12 | The organization shall establish a flexible improvement framework that allows managers to select appropriate techniques — from structured project-based approaches to rapid iterative methods — depending on the nature and urgency of the improvement challenge |

## Related Policies
<!-- sources: ITIL 4 Continual Improvement §2.3 Table 2.1 -->

| Related Policy | Relationship |
|----------------|-------------|
| Change Management Policy (PR15) | Improvement initiatives requiring changes to services or infrastructure are implemented through change management. Requests for change are a key output of the continual improvement practice |
| Measurement & Reporting Policy (PR20) | Provides tools, metrics, and baseline data for assessing current state and measuring improvement results. Service reports are a primary source of improvement opportunities |
| Service Level Management Policy (PR02) | SLM practice reviews identify service quality issues that become improvement opportunities. Improvements affecting service consumers are agreed through SLM |
| Risk Management Policy (PR21) | Risk assessments may identify improvement needs. Risks associated with improvement initiatives are assessed through risk management |
| Service Portfolio Management Policy (PR01) | Major improvement initiatives require portfolio-level funding decisions and business case approval |
| Supplier Management Policy (PR23) | Joint improvement initiatives with suppliers are negotiated through supplier management. Supply chain improvements require supplier collaboration |
| Relationship Management Policy (PR22) | Joint improvement initiatives with customers are negotiated through relationship management. Customer feedback is a key source of improvement ideas |
| Knowledge Management Policy (PR19) | Lessons learned from improvement initiatives feed the knowledge management system. Knowledge sharing is critical to the success of continual improvement |
