# Knowledge Management — Best Practice RACI Matrix

## How to Read This Matrix

- **R** — Responsible: performs the work
- **A** — Accountable: owns the outcome (exactly one per activity)
- **C** — Consulted: provides input before the activity
- **I** — Informed: notified after the activity

Knowledge management involves many organizational roles because knowledge is created, used, and shared across all practices and value streams. The three roles below consolidate the practice-specific responsibilities described in ITIL 4 Table 4.3. In practice, the knowledge management team is distributed — team members are identified in every organizational team (product, functional, management) rather than being isolated in a dedicated formal structure.

## Notes

1. **Knowledge Manager:** The central coordinating role for the knowledge management practice. Can be a dedicated person or the responsibilities can be taken by a person or team responsible for a specific knowledge management practice area. The role should focus less on coordinating and completing actions through knowledge asset management, and more on creating a knowledge management culture and competencies for the organization's evolution. Responsibilities include: coordinating KM culture and capabilities building, defining and assigning knowledge team roles, ensuring knowledge asset management runs in relevant SVS parts, empowering and mentoring the knowledge team, formally communicating decisions through the practice lifecycle, monitoring and reviewing activities, conducting regular and ad hoc practice analyses, developing the organization's expertise in KM methods, creating an environment permeated by psychological safety, mutual respect, and trust, and holding the knowledge team accountable and empowering commitment. Competency profile: LACMT.
2. **Knowledge Management Team Member:** Part of a shared leadership team of people with different competencies who work together to achieve KM outcomes. The role can be assigned to people across the organization depending on experience, competencies, and specific projects/processes/tasks. The team together with the knowledge manager is responsible for the definition, communication, and execution of KM strategy, plans, and guidelines. It is expected that the team will apply its ingenuity and contribute ideas to the effective knowledge management practice across the organization. Competency profiles vary by activity: CAT for cultural assessment, AC for requirements review, ATC for research and data collection, ACT for information processing, MTC for knowledge integration, AT for asset discovery and classification, ACT for guideline development, C for guideline assignment, ATMC for asset review and improvement.
3. **Knowledge Asset Owner:** Not a single dedicated role but a responsibility held by any specialist or manager in the organization who accepts responsibility for the management of specific knowledge assets within their domain. The specialist team or person reviews the assignment and accepts or rejects it — rejections should be explained in enough detail to facilitate reanalysis and reassignment. If the assignment is accepted, the responsible party follows the management guideline as part of their usual work in the context of value streams and practices where the information asset is used. Competency profile: AT.
4. **Distributed Practice Model:** The most common and effective organizational model is to identify the people responsible for knowledge management in every team — product, functional, or management. The knowledge management practice should be integrated into every activity and every practice, instead of being kept isolated within a dedicated formal structure. Formal knowledge management teams and dedicated knowledge manager roles are more common in larger organizations or where the practice is mature and the importance of knowledge assets is recognized.
5. **Other Participating Roles:** Many organizational roles participate in knowledge management activities without being practice-specific roles. These include management team members, HR directors, service and product owners, relationship managers, supplier managers, information security teams, risk managers, financial managers, continual improvement managers, organizational change managers, business analysts, and technical specialists. Their involvement varies by activity and is governed by their own practices and responsibilities.
